## What Went Well

- **Adversarial review caught real, ship-blocking issues before merge**, not after: 6.2's review alone surfaced 24 findings including a genuine SSRF redirect-following hole (ADR-6.2-08), an unsafe non-transaction-scoped advisory lock, and a `security_alerts` polymorphic-payload bug that predates this epic (Story 3.4) but would have started silently dropping rows the moment a second alert type existed. All 24 were resolved before the story left `review`.
- **6.1's own Dev Notes correctly seeded 6.2 and 6.2's correctly seeded 6.3** — the `monitoredServiceHealth` dashboard stub, the `service_endpoints` vs `payment_records` table split, and the "degraded" status definition were all handed off cleanly as explicit ADRs rather than silent assumptions. When 6.3 needed a contract from 6.2 that didn't exist yet at draft time, the team caught it with a dedicated realignment pass rather than shipping a story built on a guess.
- **Consistent security posture**: RLS on every new table, SSRF connection-pinning that closes a genuine DNS-rebinding TOCTOU gap, fail-closed audit writes (including system-initiated ones via a newly-consolidated `writeSystemAuditRow`), and URL redaction for credential-bearing query params before they hit any API response or audit log.
- **Real dev-time deadlocks were found and fixed** in 6.3's own test suite (test-transaction-scoping issues), not deferred as flaky-test noise.

## What Didn't Go Well / Gaps, Debt & Risks

### 1. [Critical] Product Surface Contract gap for monitored-asset CRUD was never closed
Story 6.1's own PSC table said: *"no story in epics.md Epic 6 currently has explicit ACs for a services/certificates/domains management UI... flag it at Epic 6 sprint-planning/retrospective time so a UI story gets added before epic-6: done."* This retro is that flag being cashed — a day+ late, but here. `apps/web/src/routes` has zero routes for services/certificates/domains management; the feature is API-only with no evaluator path. `dashboard-copy.ts:19`'s `"Add first service - available in Epic 6"` is now a stale promise: Epic 6 shipped, and the promised UI didn't. This is a direct G2 gate #2 violation (every PRD user journey must be shipped, honestly placeholdered, **or** explicitly deferred with a linked follow-up story — it was none of the three until today).
**Resolution (Nestor, this retro):** open a closure story (`6-4`, following the 2.8/3.4/5.5 pattern) rather than accept-as-scope or defer-again.

### 2. [High] Story-status / sprint-status drift has recurred and spread
`6-1` and `6-2`'s story-file `Status:` header still reads `review` while `sprint-status.yaml` says `done` (P3 rule violation). This is the *exact* defect class Epic 4's retro (CP4-4) found and fixed once already. It has since recurred independently in **all three Epic 7 stories** and **Story 8-1** — 6 story files across 3 epics are drifted right now. This isn't a one-off slip; it's a pattern with no automated guard, only caught when a retro happens to sweep for it — and retros have been running behind delivery.

### 3. [High] The retrospective cadence itself is the biggest risk, not any one story
Epic 6 was functionally done 2026-07-05; by 2026-07-06 (today), Epic 7 fully completed (3/3) and Epics 8 and 9 both progressed, all without Epic 6's lessons ever reaching them. Epic 5's own retro predicted this exact failure mode: *"Six epics simultaneously in-progress is a real, growing pattern... worth a deliberate sequencing conversation before it compounds further."* One epic later, it has compounded further, not been addressed — items #1 and #2 above are the direct evidence (the same PSC-"TBD" pattern and the same status-drift pattern both recurring downstream before this retro could interrupt them).

### 4. [Medium] `AuditEvent` dual-listing fragility keeps getting reproduced, not fixed
Flagged as debt in 6.2's own adversarial review: the object + type-union double-listing pattern is "easy to silently under-cover" (a missing entry in either half is a type error only, not a runtime failure), and the story "does nothing to consolidate... just repeats the same fragile pattern a second time (after 6.1)." 6.3 adds a third occurrence. No consolidation has been scheduled anywhere.

### 5. [Medium] `payment_records` naming debt widens the domain-language gap
6.1's architecture-conflict resolution named the physical table `payment_records` while keeping the `/services` route path and all user-facing "services" language. 6.3's adversarial review called this out directly: the health dashboard and public status page both surface "services" sourced from a table named `payment_records`, and the story "doubles down on that mismatch rather than flagging it for eventual rename." Two epics have now built directly on top of the mismatched name.

### 6. [Medium] 6.3 paid a real rework tax for being drafted ahead of its dependency
6.3 was originally speculative against a `payment_records`/`getServiceHealthStatuses()` contract that didn't exist in shipped code yet, caught by its own adversarial review (16 findings, 2 critical — including the story being marked `ready-for-dev` while self-documenting a hard block on unstarted work). The subsequent realignment pass to Story 6.2's real `service_endpoints` model then needed *its own* adversarial review (14 more findings, 1 critical — a stale `payment_records` reference that directly contradicted the realignment's own "everything updated" claim). All 30 findings across both passes were resolved, but that's two full review cycles spent on a sequencing problem that Team Agreement P5-2 (from Epic 5's retro, adopted 2026-07-05 — after 6.3's first draft) is designed to prevent going forward.

### 7. [Low] Epic 5's newly-adopted P5-3 agreement has its first live test case, sitting right here
P5-3: *"Product Surface Contract 'TBD' decisions... should be resolved when the story is created, not carried as an open question until the epic retrospective."* 6.1 predates that agreement, so it isn't a violation — but finding #1 above is exactly the scenario P5-3 exists to prevent. Closing it today is the agreement's first real proof point.

### 8. [Low] Several small, individually-reasonable public-surface trade-offs compound on `status_pages`
No abuse/enumeration visibility beyond standard access logs, a shared (not per-token) per-IP rate-limit bucket that can lock out legitimate NAT'd viewers, and no CORS/embeddability guidance — each was explicitly accepted as a v1 trade-off in 6.3's adversarial review, but the compounding effect on a public, unauthenticated, externally-shareable surface was only acknowledged, not mitigated.

## Team Agreements (carried forward + new)

1. (Carried from Epic 4/5) Cross-epic requirements written in one story's Dev Notes for a future story must become that future story's explicit acceptance criteria.
2. (Carried from Epic 4/5) A story touching authorization, session lifecycle, or destructive/irreversible state changes gets a formal adversarial review — applied consistently. Epic 6 is further proof this works (30 findings caught and resolved across 6.2/6.3 alone).
3. (Carried from Epic 5) A "flag it, don't do it" deferral must be paired with a tracked backlog entry, not just prose — Epic 6's own PSC flag is the case study for why, having sat unconverted since 2026-07-03.
4. (New) Don't let a new epic's first story start while the previous epic's retrospective is still `optional` without an explicit, logged decision to defer it.

## Key Takeaways

1. Epic 6's backend engineering quality is high — RLS, SSRF hardening, fail-closed audit, and cross-story ADR handoffs were all executed well, and adversarial review is clearly earning its keep (30 findings caught across just 6.2/6.3).
2. The process gap, not the code, is this epic's real finding: a PSC "TBD" flag and a status-header sync issue both sat unresolved long enough for three more epics to start without them being addressed — proving Epic 5's own retro prediction about parallel-epic risk correct.
3. "Flag it in Dev Notes" continues to be an unreliable deferral mechanism on its own — every case examined across Epics 4, 5, and 6 where a flag stayed as prose rather than a backlog entry eventually needed a dedicated retrospective or closure story to surface it again.
